    • How Serious it is?

    A new report recently released by the World Health Organisation (WHO) shows that road traffic deaths continue to rise, killing 1.35 million people each year; road traffic injuries in particular are the leading cause of death for children and adolescents aged 5 to 29.

    Road safety is an issue that simply doesn't get the attention it deserves - it really is one of the great opportunities we have to save lives around the world." So this website will show the number of road deaths in the world from 1990 to 2019 in the form of data that will hopefully help countries implement strong policies and enforcement so that smart road design, and strong public awareness campaigns can save millions of lives in the decades to come.

    This content is based around a discussion of which year A country with a lot of deaths doesn't necessarily have a high death rate, it's all about population. There are notes about key countries to help you to read them.

    Traffic Deaths Around the World

    World Road Number of Deaths 1990-2019 (Figure 1)

    The total number of road deaths in the world is very high. This chart records the number of road traffic fatalities for almost 30 years from 1990 to 2019. Since 1990, China has had the highest number of road deaths in the world at 230,499, and in 2019, still with 25,025 deaths, it continues to be number one.

    Meanwhile, with the establishment of road safety laws, Russia has dropped from the third highest level of fatalities, from 39,298 in 1990, to 20,867 in 2019. This is profound proof that as time goes on and civilisation progresses, people will become safer and safer.

    And since 2019 is the closest in time and has the highest number of deaths, the next are studied around 2019.

    Top 20 Countries in Terms of Road Deaths in 2019 (Figure 2)

    Based on the above information, this content selects the top 20 countries with the highest 2019 fatality numbers and compares them side-by-side.

    As can be seen, China is far ahead of the following countries in terms of road deaths, coming in first with a figure of 25,025. second place goes to India with 21,975. when using the Range Selection tool, the differences between countries can be seen more clearly and when moving to 42,000, only Brazil, India and China will remain. Although Brazil has a small population, it is still third in the world in 2019 in terms of fatalities, which is something that needs to be continuously improved for the country's road safety

    Top 10 Most Populous Countries in 2019 (Figure 3)

    Moreover, the number of road deaths and the size of the country's population are also closely related. These are the 10 countries with the highest population numbers in 2019.

    Of these 10 countries, China, with the highest population, is followed by countries such as India and Indonesia.

    When there are too many people, perhaps more deaths are produced, in which Russia, which has the smallest population compared to the others, also has 20,867 traffic deaths.

    Top 10 Population Road Death Rate in 2019 (Figure 4)

    Secondly, the number of road deaths and the size of the country's population are also closely related. These are the 10 countries with the highest population numbers in 2019.

    Of these 10 countries, the highest population and highest number of deaths was China, but surprisingly Brazil was first in terms of deaths per million and mortality rate. China has a road fatality rate of 0.0174, with 174 deaths per million, while Brazil has a fatality rate of 0.0211, with almost 212 deaths per million.

    Combining information from the internet with Brazil's national context, the Braziian government may be able to develop a road management strategy to reduce the number of road deaths among the Brazilian people.

    China India and Brazil

    China India and Brazil Road Deaths per Million from 1990-2019 (Figure 5)

    Finally, the three countries with the highest mortality rates are compared on a linear graph. The vertical coordinate is ROAD deaths per million and the horizontal coordinate is 1990-2019. million.

    This graph shows that although China was previously shown to be first in overall deaths, averaged out per million, the number of deaths in Brazil is close to the sum of deaths in China and India in 2019. China, on the other hand, peaked in 2004 with an average of 247 deaths per million people.

    And because of the development of civilisation, the spread of technology and the beginning of road safety controls in various countries, the average number of deaths per million in these three countries is also slowly decreasing after 2010.

    Conclusion

    The above also provides profound proof of the main point of our discussion, that countries with higher death tolls do not necessarily have higher mortality rates. When we set the number of people at one million, Brazil, which ranked 8th in 2019, was at one point the country with the highest mortality rate.

    So when we look at the number of deaths, we have to take into account the population base of the country. At the same time, as culture advances, technology develops and laws improve, the road fatality rate in every country is gradually decreasing every year and we hope that every driver and pedestrian will respect the rules of the road and cherish life.


    According to the World Health Organisation, road traffic injuries caused an estimated 1.35 million deaths worldwide in 2016.

    That is, one person died every 25 seconds. Only 28 countries, representing 449 million people (7% of the world's population), have appropriate laws for all five risk factors (speeding, drink driving, helmets, seat belts and child restraints). In low- and middle-income countries, more than a third of road traffic deaths are among pedestrians and bicyclists. Yet less than 35% of low- and middle-income countries have policies in place to protect these road users. The global average road traffic fatality rate is 17.4 per 100,000 people, with a high rate of 24.1 in low-income countries and only 9.2 in high-income countries.

    74% of road traffic fatalities occur in middle-income countries, which account for only 53% of the world's registered vehicles. The situation is worse in low-income countries, where only 1% of the world's registered vehicles are responsible for 16% of road traffic deaths. These countries are responsible for a disproportionate number of road traffic fatalities.————Wikipedia
